Michigan Debt-to-Income Ratio [2026]: HFA Overlays, 43% QM Rule, and DTI Breaking Points

State-specific rules, federal court data, and practical guidance for Michigan residents.

Michigan Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI) Reference

Debt-to-income ratio (DTI) is the single most-used number in Michigan mortgage, auto, and credit-card underwriting. It has two flavors:

  • Front-end DTI: Monthly housing cost (PITI) / gross monthly income. Mortgage lenders watch this for affordability.
  • Back-end DTI: Total monthly debt payments (PITI + auto + student + credit card minimums + alimony/child support) / gross monthly income. This is the big one.

Michigan Median Income and DTI Reference Lines

Median household income in Michigan is approximately $68,500/year, or $5,708/month (ACS 2022).

  • 28% front-end housing cap for median-income Michigan household: $1,598/month.
  • 43% back-end cap (QM safe harbor): $2,454/month total debt payments.
  • Average Michigan credit card balance: $5,900. Minimum at 2% = $118/month; this alone consumes 2.1% of median monthly income.

This is why Michigan households with above-average credit card balances push rapidly into mortgage-disqualifying DTI, even without a car note.

Michigan HFA / First-Time Homebuyer DTI Overlay

Michigan state housing finance agencies (HFAs) have their own DTI overlays that may be more or less lenient than FHA/conventional. Current Michigan overlay:

MSHDA Flex: 45% DTI; 640 min.

HFA programs pair generous DTI with down-payment assistance, so a Michigan household near 43-50% back-end DTI should check HFA options before assuming mortgage-unqualified status.

What to Do When Michigan DTI Is Above 50%

Back-end DTI above 50% means most conventional relief tools (refi, HELOC, consolidation loan) are closed off. Options by severity:

  1. 50-57%: VA-loan refi (if eligible); HFA assistance; nonprofit credit counseling DMP. Budget restructure.
  2. 57-65%: DMP aggressive; debt settlement selectively; Chapter 13 for auto cram-down or mortgage arrears (if applicable).
  3. >65%: Bankruptcy territory. Means test to determine Ch 7 vs. Ch 13.

The "Means Test" Is Income-Based, Not DTI-Based

Important: the federal bankruptcy means test (11 U.S.C. 707(b)) screens on income (6-month average vs. Michigan median), not DTI. So a Michigan household with 80% DTI but below-median income still qualifies for Chapter 7; a below-median DTI with above-median income might be pushed into Chapter 13.

The Michigan median-income thresholds are updated semi-annually by the UST. See means test explainer.
